    Mathematical model — Spatial SEIR ILM

    Transmission: S → E

    Standard kernel:
    P(i exposed) = 1 − exp( −(β/N) × Σⱼ dᵢⱼ⁻ᵅ )

    COVID-19 dual-kernel (aerosol + droplet):
    P(i exposed) = 1 − exp( −(β/N) × Σⱼ [0.5·dᵢⱼ⁻⁰·⁸ + 0.5·dᵢⱼ⁻²·⁵] )

    β = transmission intensity  |  N = population size (normalization)  |  α = spatial decay  |  dᵢⱼ = Euclidean distance between i and j

    Disease timeline (current disease)

    PhaseDurationNotes
    E → I: day counter reaches incubationDays (deterministic)
    I → R: day counter reaches infectiousDays (deterministic — no geometric distribution)

    Transmission efficiency (empirical secondary cases)

    TE = Σ secondaryCount[p] / |completedNonSeedCases|

    Denominator: all individuals in R who were NOT seed cases (Y₀ injections excluded).
    Numerator: total infections caused by those individuals.
    Infector attribution: weighted random selection proportional to distance-kernel contribution.

    Assumptions

    • Discrete-time stochastic simulation (daily time steps)
    • Household spatial clustering (4 members per household, Gaussian placement)
    • Distance-dependent transmission — no explicit contact network
    • Deterministic infectious period (day counter, not geometric)
    • Presymptomatic infectiousness modelled within E state
    • Homogeneous susceptibility within population
    • No vital dynamics (births/deaths)

    Limitations

    • No explicit social network or contact structure
    • No age-stratified susceptibility or severity
    • No vaccination, immunity waning, or reinfection
    • No behavioural adaptation or NPIs modelled
    • No viral load heterogeneity
    • Transmission Efficiency ≠ Rₜ (no generation interval assumed)
    • Single strain only — no variant competition
    Model class: This is a spatial individual-level model (ILM) — not a classical compartmental ODE model and not a full agent-based model. It does not include workplaces, schools, mobility, or behavioural change. It is appropriate for educational demonstration of spatial epidemic dynamics and household-level clustering effects.

    The amber Exposed stage has two phases: silent incubation (not contagious) followed by a presymptomatic window (contagious but no symptoms yet). This is why outbreaks are hard to contain — spread happens before anyone knows they're sick.
